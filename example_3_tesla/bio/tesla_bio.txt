Nikola Tesla: The Visionary of Electricity

Early Life and Education:
Nikola Tesla was born on July 10, 1856, in Smiljan, in the Austrian Empire (modern-day Croatia). He was the fourth of five children. His father, Milutin Tesla, was an Eastern Orthodox priest, and his mother, Đuka Mandić, was an inventor of household appliances. Tesla studied at the Austrian Polytechnic in Graz and the University of Prague. He had an incredible memory and was able to perform complex calculus in his head.

Professional Career:
In 1882, Tesla began working for the Continental Edison Company in Paris. In 1884, he moved to New York City to work for Thomas Edison at his Edison Machine Works. However, the two soon clashed over their differing views on electricity—Edison favored Direct Current (DC), while Tesla advocated for Alternating Current (AC).

The War of Currents:
After leaving Edison, Tesla partnered with George Westinghouse to promote AC power. This led to the "War of Currents," a fierce competition between Edison's DC system and the Tesla-Westinghouse AC system. AC eventually proved superior for long-distance transmission and became the standard for the modern power grid.

Inventions and Discoveries:
Tesla was a prolific inventor, holding over 300 patents. His inventions include:
- The AC induction motor and transformer
- The Tesla coil, used in radio technology
- The magnifying transmitter
- Remote control (demonstrated with a radio-controlled boat in 1898)
- Early X-ray imaging (which he called shadowgraphs)
- Neon and fluorescent lighting

Wardenclyffe and Wireless Dreams:
One of Tesla's most ambitious projects was the Wardenclyffe Tower on Long Island, which he intended to use for wireless global communication and the transmission of free electrical energy. However, due to lack of funding, the project was never completed and the tower was eventually demolished.

Later Years and Eccentricities:
In his later years, Tesla became increasingly eccentric. He lived in various New York hotels, eventually settling in Room 3327 of the New Yorker Hotel. He was obsessed with the number 3 and had a deep affection for pigeons, particularly a specific white female pigeon. He claimed to have received signals from space and spoke of "death rays" and other futuristic technologies.

Legacy:
Nikola Tesla died on January 7, 1943, in poverty and relative isolation. Despite this, his contributions to science and technology are immeasurable. He predicted the development of the internet, smartphones, and wireless technology long before they became a reality. He famously said, "The present is theirs; the future, for which I have worked, is mine."

Personal Traits:
- Obsessed with cleanliness and the number 3.
- Hated pearls and round objects.
- Spoke eight languages.
- Slept only a few hours a night.
- Remained celibate throughout his life, believing it helped his scientific focus.
- Had a long-standing feud with Thomas Edison, though they were both giants of their era.
